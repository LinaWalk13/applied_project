Summaries

Break down the content of the lecture into salient points and provide a takeaway message for the students. Ideally, the focus points of the lecture are presented in the beginning, between subtopics and in the end.

How can this be done?

In the beginning of the lecture, stating the:
  1. the purpose of the lecture.

  3. knowledge that is expected to be taken away from the lecture.
 
Using midway summaries that:
  1. are built on the main elements of the lecture.
  2. review and repeat the information presented in a condensed manner.
 
Ending with:
  1. a summary of the salient points.

How does this help my students?

1. structure can focus the student's attention on salient points
2. structure can guide note-taking
3. midway summaries can provide a rest point to digest the information that have been delivered so far
4. lecture slides and lecture videos can be used to study and prepare for exams

Cognitive psychology theories supporting this point

Chunking

In cognitive psychology, chunking is the process of combining individual information into meaningful units, chunks. The elements that make up a chunk have strong associations to each other, and the number of elements in a chunk can differ. The process of chunking has been established as a key process in humans cognition and memory, with chunks being the appropriate measure of the capacity of human memory. Thus, when pieces of information are associated in a meaningful way, encoding can be enhanced and made more efficient.

Focused attention

Focused attention is a mechanism in the early stages of information processing. It is the ability to intentionally concentrate on a target stimuli, or in the context of lectures, on target information. Attention can be divided into stimuli-driven and goal-directed attention. While stimulus-driven attention is captured by the features of a stimuli such as a high-pitched sound, goal-directed attention functions on the basis of cues, these being either internal or external instructions.
Therefore, stating the salient points of a lecture provides the students with cues to where the mental focus should be directed, which is essential for eliciting the processing of information and storing it in memory.
